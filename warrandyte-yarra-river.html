Setting

Warrandyte State Park is not one continuous block: it is a 680-hectare network of reserves along roughly 11 km of river, including Pound Bend, Jumping Creek, Black Flat, Fourth Hill and Mount Lofty. The landscape is defined by a rocky gorge, steep forested slopes and entrenched river bends; Pound Bend is one of the largest entrenched meanders in a Victorian river.

The state park was announced in 1973. The first 135 hectares at Pound Bend, Jumping Creek and Black Flat were formally reserved in 1975, and later additions created the larger park managed today.

A living cultural landscape

The park is on the traditional Country of the Wurundjeri Woi-wurrung people, and the river is central to Wurundjeri identity, culture and wellbeing. The name Birrarung is commonly translated as “river of mists and shadows”. Parks Victoria recognises the deep and continuing connection of Wurundjeri Traditional Owners to these lands and waters and their ongoing role in caring for Country.

At Pound BendThe Wurundjeri Stories trail presents Wurundjeri-led accounts of culture, history and connection to this place. This is not simply a preface to the gold-mining story; it is the longer, continuing history of the river landscape.

Where Victoria’s gold rush began

Andersons Creek at Warrandyte was the site of Victoria’s first officially recognised gold discovery in 1851 and one of the colony’s first sanctioned goldfields. Mining reshaped the surrounding hills and gullies; tunnels, shafts, stopes and regenerated areas of previously cleared forest remain visible. Parks Victoria identifies Geraghty’s Mine, Victory Mine and the Evelyn Tunnel among the prominent surviving features.

The Pound Bend Tunnel

The best-known relic is the Pound Bend Gold Diversion Tunnel, also called the Evelyn Tunnel. In 1870, the Evelyn Tunnelling and Mining Company cut 145 metres through hard rock across the narrow neck of the bend. Diverting the river through the tunnel exposed about five kilometres of the former channel for alluvial mining.

The venture did not produce the expected return and the company closed in 1872, but the tunnel continues to carry the river. It is protected on the Victorian Heritage Register as a well-preserved example of early gold-mining river diversion.

Landscape & wildlife

Manna gums dominate parts of the riverbank, with wattles and box eucalypts across the slopes. Riparian forest, dry woodland, shrubland, wetlands and grassland make the park an important habitat corridor between Melbourne’s north-eastern suburbs and larger forested areas such as Kinglake National Park.

The 2006 Parks Victoria management plan recorded 202 native terrestrial fauna species—29 mammals, 144 birds, 18 reptiles and 11 frogs—plus 13 native fish species in the river and tributaries. These numbers are a historical survey baseline rather than a complete current inventory, but they demonstrate the diversity supported by the fragmented bushland.

Visiting the river

Pound Bend has picnic facilities, toilets, a canoe launch and the gentle 2.4 km Pound Bend River Walk. Other walks explore Jumping Creek, Mount Lofty and the gold-mining landscape around Fourth Hill. Canoe and kayak routes include Grade I–III rapids, depending on the section and water level, so paddlers need suitable experience and safety equipment.

Check before you goConditions can change quickly. Check Parks Victoria for closures, fire danger, parking constraints and river advice; follow signs and avoid entering closed mine workings or the diversion tunnel.
