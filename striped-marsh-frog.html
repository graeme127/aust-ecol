Striped Marsh Frog

Limnodynastes peronii
All profiles Striped marsh frog Warrandyte river Melbourne mangroves

A familiar frog of eastern Australia, recognised by dark stripes and a repeated single “tok”. It often persists in urban ponds, wetlands and drains, but its adaptability does not mean those habitats are healthy for frogs generally.

Appearance

Adults reach about 75 mm in body length. The brown back carries several dark longitudinal stripes; some individuals also have a pale cream or reddish stripe along the centre. A pale stripe runs from below the eye to the top of the forelimb. The belly is white, while breeding males have a pale yellow throat with brown mottling. The eyes have a horizontal pupil and gold iris, and the fingers and toes lack webbing and enlarged discs.

CALL
A short, explosive “tok”, “whuck” or “pop”, repeated every few seconds. Males may call from vegetation or concealed sites by day and from the water’s surface at night. Calling can extend beyond the main breeding season in some regions.

Life cycle

Breeding occurs mainly from spring to autumn, with timing varying across the range. Eggs are laid in a floating foam mass, often among vegetation at the edge of still water. Tadpoles can reach 9.5 cm in total length and may take seven to eight months to metamorphose. A breeding pond therefore needs to hold water long enough for development to finish.

Habitat & why it thrives near people

The species breeds in still or slow-moving water—ponds, marshes, swamps, slow streams, drainage channels and garden fishponds. It often persists in disturbed urban and agricultural landscapes and has been recorded breeding in water bodies that are unsuitable for more sensitive frog species. By day, adults commonly shelter beneath logs, rocks, leaf litter or other debris.

Telling it apart

Most similar Limnodynastes marsh frogs are spotted rather than striped. The salmon-striped burrowing frog (L. salmini) has salmon-pink or orange stripes, whereas L. peronii has dark brown stripes. A recording of the call is often more useful than colour alone.

CARE
Observe without handling, never move frogs or spawn between water bodies, and keep detergents, pesticides and other chemicals away from frog habitat. Recordings can be submitted to the Australian Museum’s FrogID project.
