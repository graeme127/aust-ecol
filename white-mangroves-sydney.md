# Grey (or White) Mangroves around Melbourne

*Species and habitat profile*

## One Victorian mangrove, two common names

Melbourne’s “white mangrove” is the tree also called the **grey mangrove**, *Avicennia marina*. The accepted name for the Victorian subspecies is ***Avicennia marina* subsp. *australasica***; the older “var. *australasica*” still appears in some reports and field guides.

The names describe its pale appearance: the bark is grey and the undersides of the leaves are densely covered with fine grey-white hairs. Salt secreted through the leaves can add a crystalline coating. Grey mangrove belongs to the family Acanthaceae and is the **only mangrove species naturally occurring in Victoria**.

## Where Melbourne’s mangroves grow

Grey mangrove occupies sheltered tidal mudflats around **Port Phillip Bay and Western Port**, where regular inundation, fine sediment and protection from strong waves create suitable habitat. VicFlora records it as locally common from the western half of Port Phillip Bay east to Corner Inlet.

The most accessible metropolitan example is **Jawbone Marine Sanctuary beside Williamstown**, about 20 minutes from central Melbourne. It protects the largest mangrove area within Port Phillip and Victoria’s only mangroves growing on a basalt coast. Other Port Phillip stands occur around Point Cook, Cheetham Wetlands and Avalon–Limeburners Lagoon.

The most extensive and well-developed Victorian mangrove communities occur in **Western Port**, including French Island, Yaringa, Churchill Island, Hastings, Lang Lang and Grantville. Victoria’s mangroves are among the world’s most southerly; the southernmost occurrence is recorded at Corner Inlet.

Victorian plants are usually compact—a **shrub or small tree about 1–4 metres high**—rather than the much taller trees found in warmer parts of the species’ range.

## Built for salt, mud and tides

- **Breathing roots:** shallow lateral roots produce hundreds of upright, pencil-like **pneumatophores**. Air enters through pores in these roots when the tide is out, supplying oxygen to tissues buried in waterlogged, oxygen-poor mud.
- **Salt regulation:** roots limit salt uptake, while specialised glands on the leaves excrete excess salt. This allows the tree to live in saline water, although growth is often strongest in brackish conditions.
- **Sediment capture:** roots slow water movement, trap fine sediment and help stabilise muddy shorelines. Sediment trapping can also raise the wetland surface and change the boundary between mangrove and saltmarsh.

## Flowers, fruit and dispersal

Grey mangrove produces small white-to-yellow flowers, often during much of the year in Victoria. Its reproduction is described as **cryptovivipary**: the embryo begins developing while the fruit is still attached to the parent, but remains enclosed by the fruit wall until dispersal. The buoyant fruit can float with the tide, shed its outer covering and establish after becoming lodged on suitable wet mud.

This is more precise than simply calling the species viviparous. In “true” viviparous mangroves such as *Rhizophora*, the developing seedling visibly grows out through the fruit before it falls.

## Why Melbourne’s mangroves matter

Mangrove stands are productive links between land and estuary. They:

- provide shelter, feeding grounds and nursery habitat for fish and crustaceans;
- support crabs, molluscs, insects and birds, including migratory shorebirds;
- trap sediment and nutrients, reduce erosion and influence water quality; and
- store **blue carbon**, especially in their waterlogged soils.

These benefits depend on the condition and connectivity of the entire coastal wetland—including adjacent mudflats, saltmarsh and seagrass—not on mangroves alone. Western Port’s internationally recognised Ramsar wetland is a particularly important example of this habitat mosaic.

## A restricted and changing habitat

Victoria’s mangroves are estimated to cover about **2,313 hectares**. Despite being locally common at suitable sites, *Avicennia marina* subsp. *australasica* is listed as **Endangered under Victoria’s Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act** because its habitat is restricted and has been substantially modified.

Historical clearing and harvesting, coastal development, altered sediment movement, invasive species and erosion have affected mangrove communities. Climate change adds a different pressure: as sea level rises, mangroves and saltmarsh need room to migrate landward. Roads, seawalls, levees and other built structures can block that movement and squeeze coastal wetlands against the shoreline.

Mangroves can also expand into adjacent saltmarsh as water levels, sediment and climate change. That shift should not automatically be described as ecological recovery or degradation: both habitats have distinct values and need to be managed as a connected system.

# Grey (or White) Mangroves in Sydney

*Species and habitat profile*

## One tree, two common names

Sydney’s “white mangrove” is the tree more commonly called the **grey mangrove**, *Avicennia marina*. The accepted name for the local subspecies is ***Avicennia marina* subsp. *australasica***; the older combination “var. *australasica*” still appears in some field guides and vegetation maps.

Both common names describe its pale appearance: the bark is grey and the undersides of the leaves are densely covered with fine grey-white hairs. Salt secreted through the leaves can add a visible crystalline coating. Grey mangrove belongs to the family Acanthaceae. Sydney’s other widespread mangrove, the river mangrove (*Aegiceras corniculatum*), is usually a smaller, multi-stemmed shrub or tree.

## Where it grows

Grey mangrove forms forests and fringing stands along sheltered tidal rivers, creeks, estuaries and bays. Around Sydney it is prominent along the **Parramatta River**, **Lane Cove River**, Sydney Harbour and Botany Bay. It usually grows in the intertidal zone where fine sediments accumulate and salt water mixes with freshwater runoff.

Trees around Sydney are commonly **3–10 metres tall**. The subspecies extends from Malesia through eastern and south-eastern Australia to northern New Zealand and reaches some of the most southerly mangrove habitat in the world.

## Built for salt, mud and tides

- **Breathing roots:** shallow lateral roots produce hundreds of upright, pencil-like **pneumatophores**. Air enters through pores in these roots when the tide is out, supplying oxygen to tissues buried in waterlogged, oxygen-poor mud.
- **Salt regulation:** roots limit salt uptake, and specialised glands on the leaves excrete excess salt. This allows the tree to live in saline water, although growth is often strongest in brackish conditions.
- **Sediment capture:** roots slow water movement, trap fine sediment and help stabilise muddy shorelines. The same sediment trapping can gradually raise the wetland surface and change where mangrove and saltmarsh plants grow.

## Flowers, fruit and dispersal

Grey mangrove produces small cream-to-yellow flowers that are visited by insects. Its reproduction is described as **cryptovivipary**: the embryo begins developing while the fruit is still attached to the parent, but it remains enclosed by the fruit wall until dispersal. The buoyant fruit can float with the tide, shed its outer covering and establish when it becomes lodged on suitable wet mud.

This is more precise than simply calling the species viviparous. In “true” viviparous mangroves such as *Rhizophora*, the developing seedling visibly grows out through the fruit before it falls.

## Why mangrove forests matter

Mangrove stands are highly productive links between land and estuary. They:

- provide shelter, feeding grounds and nursery habitat for fish and crustaceans;
- support crabs, molluscs, insects and birds;
- trap sediment and nutrients, reduce erosion and influence water quality; and
- store substantial **blue carbon**, especially in their waterlogged soils.

These benefits depend on the condition and connectivity of the whole estuarine wetland, including adjacent mudflats, saltmarsh and seagrass—not on mangroves alone.

## Expansion, saltmarsh and management

Mangrove cover has expanded in parts of south-eastern Australia, including some Sydney estuaries. The drivers vary by site and include altered tidal flows, sedimentation, sea-level change and warmer temperatures. Expansion can benefit mangrove habitat while reducing adjacent **coastal saltmarsh**, so it should not automatically be described as either ecological recovery or degradation.

That trade-off matters because coastal saltmarsh in the NSW North Coast, Sydney Basin and South East Corner bioregions is listed as an **endangered ecological community in NSW**. The broader subtropical and temperate coastal saltmarsh community is listed as **vulnerable nationally** under the EPBC Act.

Grey mangrove itself is common, but mangroves are protected marine vegetation in NSW. Harming them in protected areas generally requires a permit under the **Fisheries Management Act 1994**. Coastal planning controls also apply to mapped wetlands and development in the coastal zone.
