# Striped Marsh Frog

*Species profile — eastern Australia*

## At a glance

The striped marsh frog (*Limnodynastes peronii*) is a familiar frog of eastern Australia. Its repeated, single-note **“tok”** is often heard around suburban ponds, wetlands and drains. The species copes well with many modified habitats, although that adaptability should not be taken as evidence that urban waterways are healthy for frogs generally.

## Identification

Adults reach about **75 mm in body length**. The back is brown with several dark longitudinal stripes; some individuals also have a pale cream or reddish stripe along the centre. A pale stripe runs from below the eye to the top of the forelimb. The belly is white, while breeding males have a pale yellow throat with brown mottling. The eyes have a horizontal pupil and gold iris, and the fingers and toes lack webbing and enlarged discs.

Most similar marsh frogs are spotted rather than striped. The salmon-striped burrowing frog (*L. salmini*) has salmon-pink or orange stripes, whereas *L. peronii* has dark brown stripes.

## Call and behaviour

The advertisement call is a short, explosive **“tok”, “whuck” or “pop”**, repeated every few seconds. Males may call from vegetation or concealed sites by day and from the water’s surface at night. Calling can occur beyond the main breeding season in some regions, so season alone is not a reliable identification feature.

By day, adults commonly shelter under logs, rocks, leaf litter or other debris. They emerge at night to feed on insects and other small invertebrates.

## Habitat

Striped marsh frogs breed in still or slow-moving water, including ponds, marshes, swamps, slow streams, drainage channels and garden fishponds. They often persist in disturbed urban and agricultural landscapes and have been recorded breeding in water bodies that are unsuitable for more sensitive frog species.

## Breeding and development

Breeding occurs mainly from **spring to autumn**, with timing varying across the species’ broad range. Eggs are laid in a floating foam mass, often among vegetation at the edge of still water. Tadpoles can reach **95 mm in total length** and may take **seven to eight months** to metamorphose. Long larval development makes water permanence important: a breeding pond must hold water long enough for tadpoles to complete development.

## Distribution

The species occurs along the NSW coast, in the ACT, throughout coastal Victoria, in coastal Queensland south of Cape York, in south-eastern South Australia and in northern Tasmania. It is common in many parts of this range, including major cities along Australia’s east coast.

## Observing responsibly

The call is usually a better identification clue than colour alone. A short recording can be submitted to the Australian Museum’s **FrogID** project for expert-supported identification. Observe frogs without handling them, avoid moving frogs or spawn between water bodies, and keep detergents, pesticides and other chemicals away from frog habitat.
